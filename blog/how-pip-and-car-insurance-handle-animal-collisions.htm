How PIP and Car Insurance Handle Animal Collisions

  • Home
  • /
  • Blog
  • /
  • Blog Details

It happens in an instant—a flicker of movement at the edge of the headlights, a shadow darting across the road. Then, impact. Colliding with an animal is a jarring experience, both emotionally and physically. In the United States, there are over 1.5 million vehicle accidents involving large animals like deer each year, resulting in billions of dollars in damage, thousands of injuries, and sadly, hundreds of fatalities. But beyond the initial shock and vehicle damage, a complex web of insurance policies awaits. In an era of expanding urban sprawl and shifting wildlife patterns due to climate change, understanding how insurance handles these incidents is more critical than ever. This article dives deep into the roles of Personal Injury Protection (PIP) and standard car insurance when you have an unexpected encounter with the animal kingdom.

The Growing Problem: Why Animal Collisions Are a Hot-Topic Issue

The frequency of animal-vehicle collisions is not static; it's a growing concern intertwined with several contemporary global challenges.

Climate Change and Habitat Encroachment

As human development continues to expand into natural habitats, wildlife is forced to adapt. Forests are cleared for housing, and migratory corridors are bisected by highways. Compounding this is climate change, which alters animal behavior and migration patterns. Droughts, for instance, may drive animals farther to find water and food, often leading them across busy roadways. This constant push-and-pull between development and nature puts drivers and animals on a collision course more often.

The Economic and Human Toll

The financial impact is staggering. The average insurance claim for a deer collision hovers around $4,000, but with today's vehicles packed with sensitive sensors, cameras, and radar systems embedded in bumpers, a single strike can easily cause over $10,000 in damage. Beyond the financial cost is the human one. Swerving to avoid an animal can lead to more severe accidents, including rollovers or head-on collisions with other vehicles. The emotional trauma, from the death of an animal to the potential injury of passengers, is a significant but often overlooked consequence.

Your Financial Shield: The Role of Car Insurance in Animal Collisions

When you file a claim after hitting an animal, you're primarily dealing with the physical damage to your vehicle. This is where the different components of your auto policy come into play. It's crucial to understand that standard liability insurance, which is mandatory in most states, does not cover damage to your own car in an animal collision. Liability only covers damage you cause to others.

Comprehensive Coverage: Your First Line of Defense

For damage caused by hitting an animal, you need comprehensive coverage (often called "other than collision" coverage). This part of your insurance policy is designed specifically for events outside of your control that are not collisions with another vehicle. This includes: * Hitting a deer, elk, moose, or other large animal. * Your car being damaged by an animal (e.g., a bear breaking a window). * Falling objects, hail, fire, theft, and vandalism.

If you have comprehensive coverage, you will file a claim under this portion of your policy. You will be responsible for paying your deductible, and then your insurance will cover the remaining cost of repairs. This claim is generally not considered "at-fault" in most states, meaning it typically should not cause your premiums to skyrocket on its own.

Collision Coverage: When You Swerve

This is where things get tricky. If you swerve to avoid an animal and end up hitting a tree, guardrail, or another vehicle, you are now involved in an at-fault accident. In this scenario, damage to your car would be covered under your collision coverage, not comprehensive. This means you pay your collision deductible, and the incident is logged as an at-fault collision, which is more likely to affect your future insurance rates.

Protecting the People Inside: The Critical Role of PIP Insurance

While comprehensive coverage handles the car, Personal Injury Protection (PIP) is designed to handle the people. PIP is a type of "no-fault" insurance required in certain "no-fault" states like Florida, Michigan, New York, and others. Its purpose is to pay for medical expenses and related costs regardless of who caused the accident.

How PIP Kicks In After an Animal Strike

Even in a single-vehicle accident like hitting a deer, passengers can sustain injuries from the impact—whiplash being the most common, but also airbag-related injuries, broken bones, or cuts from shattered glass. This is where PIP becomes invaluable. It can cover: * Medical expenses for you and your passengers. * Lost wages if injuries prevent you from working. * Essential services, like paying for someone to clean your house if you are incapacitated. * In tragic cases, funeral expenses.

In no-fault states, you would turn to your own PIP coverage first to handle these costs, up to the policy's limits. In at-fault states without PIP, you would typically rely on your health insurance for medical bills, and potentially your MedPay coverage (a smaller, optional medical payments supplement) if you have it.

The Gaps and Challenges with PIP

PIP coverage has limits, and those limits can be exhausted quickly with serious injuries. Furthermore, the requirements and benefits vary dramatically from state to state. In some states, PIP offers robust coverage, while in others, it is minimal. This patchwork system means the financial safety net for injured drivers is inconsistent across the country, leaving some potentially vulnerable after a traumatic event.

Navigating the Aftermath: A Step-by-Step Guide

Knowing what to do immediately after a collision can protect your safety and strengthen your insurance claim.

  1. Prioritize Safety: Move your vehicle to a safe location off the road if possible. Turn on your hazard lights. Check yourself and passengers for injuries.
  2. Call the Authorities: Contact the police. A official report can be crucial documentation for your insurance company, especially if there is significant vehicle damage or injuries.
  3. Document the Scene: If it is safe to do so, take photographs of the road, your vehicle's damage, the animal, and any relevant road conditions. Note the location, time, and date.
  4. Avoid the Animal: Do not approach injured animals. They are frightened and can be dangerous.
  5. Notify Your Insurer: Contact your insurance company as soon as practicable to start the claims process. Have your policy number and the police report number ready.

Future-Proofing Your Drive: Prevention and Policy Review

The best insurance claim is the one you never have to file. Being aware is your greatest tool, especially during high-risk times like dawn and dusk in the autumn months.

  • Use High Beams: When appropriate, use high beams to illuminate the eyes of animals on the side of the road.
  • Heed Warning Signs: Animal crossing signs are placed in known high-traffic areas for wildlife. Slow down and be extra vigilant.
  • Don't Swerve: The safest maneuver, if a collision is unavoidable, is to brake firmly and stay in your lane. Swerving drastically increases the risk of a more serious accident.

Finally, the most proactive step you can take is to sit down and actually read your insurance policy. Do you have comprehensive coverage? What is your deductible? Do you live in a PIP state, and what are your limits? Understanding your coverage before an accident happens is the ultimate form of protection in our ever-changing world. It empowers you to make informed decisions and ensures that when the unexpected occurs, you and your finances are as secure as possible.
